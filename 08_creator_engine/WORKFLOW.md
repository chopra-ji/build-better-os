# Workflow — Creator Engine

This file documents all eight review stages in full. For each stage: its mission, responsibilities, the questions it asks, pass/fail criteria, the optimization checklist applied on a fail, and concrete examples of improvements.

For scoring mechanics, see `SCORING_SYSTEM.md`. For what happens when a stage fails, see `OPTIMIZATION_PIPELINE.md`. For the final production gate, see `QUALITY_STANDARDS.md`.

---

## Stage 1 — Hook Review

### Mission
Ensure the first 3 seconds of the script stop the scroll. On Instagram, 60–70% of drop-off happens in the first 3 seconds. A technically correct opening that fails to arrest attention makes every subsequent optimization irrelevant.

### Responsibilities
- Evaluate the designated hook line against attention criteria
- Identify the specific mechanism the hook uses (question, contradiction, bold claim, pattern interrupt, identity statement)
- Score hook strength from 1–10
- Optimize if below threshold

### Questions This Stage Asks
1. Does the first sentence create immediate tension, curiosity, or recognition?
2. Is the hook front-loaded — does the most compelling word or idea appear in the first 4 words?
3. Does the hook make a specific, testable claim rather than a vague one?
4. Would someone who was about to swipe stop because of this line?
5. Does the hook connect to an outcome the audience already wants?
6. Is the hook free of preamble ("In this video I'm going to tell you about...")?

### Pass Criteria
- Score ≥ 7
- Hook mechanism is identifiable
- Hook contains no preamble or throat-clearing
- First 4 words carry the weight of the hook

### Fail Criteria
- Score ≤ 6
- Hook is a statement of intent ("Today I want to talk about...")
- Hook is generic enough to describe ten other videos
- Hook buries the compelling idea after a setup

### Optimization Checklist
- [ ] Remove all preamble before the hook
- [ ] Move the most specific, arresting word or idea to the opening
- [ ] Replace vague nouns with specific ones (not "a mistake" → "the mistake that cost me $40K")
- [ ] Convert declarative hooks to question or contradiction format if they are underperforming
- [ ] Test the hook against the question: "Would I stop scrolling for this?"
- [ ] Verify the hook promises something the rest of the script delivers

### Example Improvements

**Before:** "Today I want to talk about something that really changed how I think about building an audience."

**After:** "Most creators grind for two years before they understand this. I learned it in week three."

**Why it works:** The revised hook creates a knowledge gap ("this"), introduces a competitive tension (week three vs. two years), and implies the viewer is about to learn something that shortcuts a painful process.

---

**Before:** "Here are five things I wish I knew before starting my business."

**After:** "I spent $80,000 before someone told me what I'm about to tell you for free."

**Why it works:** Specificity ($80,000) creates immediate credibility and loss aversion. "For free" triggers contrast. The implied offer creates forward pull.

## Stage 2 — Retention Review

### Mission
Ensure the script is structured to hold attention from second 4 through the final CTA. Instagram's algorithm measures average watch time and completion rate — both require engineering the middle and end of the script, not just the opening.

### Responsibilities
- Map the script's structural beats
- Identify drop-off risk points (flat exposition, slow pacing, unearned transitions)
- Verify that tension, payoff, and forward momentum are distributed throughout
- Score retention architecture from 1–10

### Questions This Stage Asks
1. Is there a re-hook — a second moment of tension or curiosity — within the first 15 seconds?
2. Does the script create a clear promise in the opening that it pays off at the end?
3. Are transitions between points smooth, or do they require the viewer to make a mental leap?
4. Are there any sections that could be removed without losing meaning?
5. Does the pacing accelerate toward the end, or does it stall?
6. Is there a reason for the viewer to stay through the CTA, or does the value feel complete before it?

### Pass Criteria
- Score ≥ 7
- Script contains at least one re-hook before the 15-second mark
- No section longer than 12 seconds without a structural beat (question, payoff, transition, or new claim)
- The opening promise is explicitly paid off before the CTA

### Fail Criteria
- Score ≤ 6
- Script has a strong hook followed by flat, sequential exposition
- Any section exceeds 15 seconds without a tension or payoff beat
- The script ends on information delivery without an emotional landing

### Optimization Checklist
- [ ] Insert a re-hook at the 10–15 second mark if none exists
- [ ] Cut or compress any section that delivers the same beat twice
- [ ] Reorder points so each one raises a question the next point answers
- [ ] Add a "bridge" line between unearned transitions
- [ ] Ensure the final 5 seconds contain both the emotional landing and the CTA
- [ ] Test: can you identify what the viewer is waiting for at every moment in the script?

### Example Improvements

**Before (flat exposition):** "First, you need to understand your audience. Second, you need a consistent posting schedule. Third, you need to pick the right format for your content."

**After (tension-threaded):** "First rule: know your audience — not their demographics, their 3am anxiety. Second rule: consistency isn't a schedule, it's a promise. Third rule: format isn't about what you like. It's about what they share. Most people get all three backwards."

**Why it works:** Each point sets up a small surprise or reframe. The final line ("Most people get all three backwards") creates a knowledge gap that makes the viewer want to rewatch or comment.

## Stage 3 — Founder Voice Review

### Mission
Ensure the script sounds like a real, specific person talking — not a content format. Instagram audiences disengage from scripts that feel templated or produced-for-the-algorithm. Founder Voice is the quality of authentic perspective and earned credibility.

### Responsibilities
- Evaluate whether the script reflects a distinct point of view
- Identify lines that sound generic, borrowed, or impersonal
- Verify that claims are grounded in experience rather than abstraction
- Score founder voice from 1–10

### Questions This Stage Asks
1. Is there at least one line in this script that only this founder could have written?
2. Does the script express an opinion, or does it only report information?
3. Are the examples specific (named, dated, numbered) or generic ("once when I...")?
4. Does the language match how the founder actually speaks, or does it sound like a content category?
5. Is there evidence of earned experience — something the founder lost, failed at, or learned the hard way?
6. Would the viewer know who made this if the name was removed?

### Pass Criteria
- Score ≥ 7
- Script contains at least one specific, personal claim (not "I learned" but "In March 2023, after losing a $12K client...")
- Script contains at least one contrarian or unexpected perspective
- No more than 20% of lines could have been written by any creator in this niche

### Fail Criteria
- Score ≤ 6
- Script reads like an aggregated best-practices list with no personal grounding
- All examples are hypothetical or generic
- Script takes no position — it only presents multiple sides without choosing one

### Optimization Checklist
- [ ] Replace any generic example with a specific, personal one
- [ ] Add a contrarian line if the script only states consensus opinions
- [ ] Replace passive information delivery ("Studies show...") with personal accountability ("I tried this for 90 days and...")
- [ ] Convert "you should" statements to "here's what I did differently" framing where applicable
- [ ] Remove any language that sounds like it came from a listicle or business article

### Example Improvements

**Before:** "Authenticity is important for building trust with your audience. People can tell when you're being fake."

**After:** "I posted polished, professional content for eight months. Zero traction. The week I posted from my car, half-asleep, ranting about a bad client call — 40,000 views."

**Why it works:** The specific detail (eight months, car, 40K views) makes the claim credible. The contrast is vivid. The message is identical — authenticity works — but one version is something only this person could have said.

## Stage 4 — Social Language Review

### Mission
Ensure the script uses language that is native to Instagram and spoken conversation, not written prose or formal business communication. Social language is shorter, more direct, more colloquial, and more rhythmic than written content.

### Responsibilities
- Identify all language that reads like written copy rather than spoken word
- Verify sentence length is appropriate for the medium
- Evaluate whether the vocabulary matches the platform register
- Score social language from 1–10

### Questions This Stage Asks
1. Does every sentence read naturally when spoken aloud?
2. Are there any sentences longer than 20 words?
3. Is the vocabulary accessible — no unnecessary jargon or formal register?
4. Does the script use contractions and informal connectors ("and," "but," "so" at the start of sentences)?
5. Does the rhythm of the language vary — short punchy sentences alternating with longer ones?
6. Are there any corporate phrases that need to be replaced ("leverage," "optimize," "synergy," "utilize")?

### Pass Criteria
- Score ≥ 7
- No sentence exceeds 25 words
- At least 80% of sentences are under 15 words
- No corporate or written-register vocabulary
- Language reads fluently when spoken at conversation pace

### Fail Criteria
- Score ≤ 6
- Script reads better silently than aloud
- Multiple sentences require re-reading to parse
- Vocabulary is drawn from formal writing registers
- Rhythm is flat — all sentences are the same length

### Optimization Checklist
- [ ] Read the entire script aloud. Mark every line that sounds unnatural when spoken.
- [ ] Break all sentences over 20 words into two or three shorter ones
- [ ] Replace all written-register vocabulary with spoken equivalents
- [ ] Add sentence rhythm variation — place at least three 1–5 word sentences in the script
- [ ] Remove or replace all filler transitions ("Additionally," "Furthermore," "In conclusion")
- [ ] Add verbal punctuation where natural ("Right." "Here's the thing." "That's it.")

### Example Improvements

**Before:** "It is important to recognize that the primary driver of audience growth on social media platforms is consistent, high-quality content creation that resonates with your target demographic."

**After:** "One thing drives growth on Instagram. Consistent content that actually connects. That's it. Everything else is noise."

**Why it works:** The revision breaks one complex sentence into four short declarative sentences. The rhythm creates emphasis. The final "That's it." is verbal punctuation that lands with authority.

## Stage 5 — Comment Engineering

### Mission
Ensure the script is designed to provoke a specific, high-value comment response. Comments signal algorithmic value on Instagram. Engineered comments — those triggered by specific lines in the script — are more reliable than hoping the content is good enough to prompt organic response.

### Responsibilities
- Identify whether the script contains a comment trigger
- Evaluate the trigger type (question, poll framing, opinion challenge, identity statement, fill-in-the-blank)
- Verify the trigger is specific enough to produce a pointed response, not a generic one
- Score comment engineering from 1–10

### Questions This Stage Asks
1. Does the script contain an explicit moment that invites the audience to respond?
2. Is the comment trigger specific enough that a viewer knows exactly what to say?
3. Does the trigger ask for an opinion, a personal story, or a yes/no that reveals identity?
4. Is the trigger placed at a high-attention moment (not buried in the middle of exposition)?
5. Does the script avoid generic CTAs ("Let me know what you think in the comments")?
6. Is the trigger tied to the core message, or is it a detached question added at the end?

### Pass Criteria
- Score ≥ 7
- Script contains at least one identifiable comment trigger
- Trigger is specific (produces a predictable type of comment, not a vague one)
- Trigger appears at a structural beat — not isolated at the end

### Fail Criteria
- Score ≤ 6
- Script has no comment trigger at all
- Script ends with "Drop a comment below" with no specific prompt
- Trigger is so vague it could produce any type of response

### Optimization Checklist
- [ ] Identify the most divisive or relatable claim in the script — place a trigger there
- [ ] Replace generic end-of-script prompts with specific questions tied to the content
- [ ] Use identity-based triggers ("If you've done this, comment X") to segment the audience
- [ ] Try fill-in-the-blank triggers embedded mid-script ("The one thing I'd tell my past self is ___")
- [ ] Verify the trigger is something the target audience has an immediate, specific answer to

### Example Improvements

**Before:** "What do you think? Let me know in the comments."

**After (mid-script trigger):** "I've asked this to every founder I've worked with. Here's what I want to know from you: what's the first thing you gave up to build this? Drop it below."

**Why it works:** The question is personal, specific, and demands reflection — not just agreement or disagreement. It also positions the audience as part of the creator's experience, not just a passive viewer.

## Stage 6 — Shareability Review

### Mission
Ensure the script contains at least one element that makes a viewer want to send it to someone specific. Shares are the highest-value engagement signal on Instagram. They happen when content creates a "this is you" or "you need to see this" moment for a third party.

### Responsibilities
- Identify the share trigger in the script
- Evaluate what emotion drives the share (recognition, provocation, aspiration, humor, alarm)
- Verify the share trigger is specific enough to map to a real person in the viewer's life
- Score shareability from 1–10

### Questions This Stage Asks
1. Is there a moment in this script that would make a viewer think of a specific person?
2. Does the script create a "this is exactly my life" moment for a defined audience?
3. Is there a surprising, counterintuitive, or quotable line that people would repeat to someone else?
4. Is the script framed so that sharing it is a social signal (identity expression, status play, genuine value to a friend)?
5. Does the share moment happen early enough that the viewer reaches it before they scroll away?

### Pass Criteria
- Score ≥ 7
- Script contains an identifiable share trigger
- Share trigger maps to a defined emotion (recognition, provocation, aspiration, humor, or alarm)
- Trigger appears within the first 40 seconds

### Fail Criteria
- Score ≤ 6
- Script delivers information but creates no "moment"
- Script is technically accurate but emotionally neutral throughout
- Share trigger, if present, is in the final 10 seconds

### Optimization Checklist
- [ ] Identify the most universal experience in the script and amplify it
- [ ] Add a "this is you" framing device ("If you've ever felt like...")
- [ ] Ensure at least one line is quotable — specific, true, and transferable out of context
- [ ] Move the strongest share trigger earlier in the script if it currently appears late
- [ ] Add contrast framing ("Everyone says X. The ones who succeed do Y.") which is inherently shareable

### Example Improvements

**Before:** "Growing a social media following takes time and consistency."

**After:** "Your first 1,000 followers will feel like they took forever. Then you'll look back and realize you weren't posting consistently. You were posting hopefully."

**Why it works:** The last line ("posting consistently vs. posting hopefully") is a quotable insight that generalizes beyond the video — viewers will send it to friends who are struggling with growth. It creates recognition and mild shame, which is a powerful share trigger.

## Stage 7 — Build Better Brand Review

### Mission
Ensure the script reflects Build Better's positioning and does not produce content that conflicts with the brand's core identity. Every piece of content is both a standalone reel and a brand impression.

### Responsibilities
- Verify the script's tone aligns with Build Better's voice
- Confirm the perspective is that of a practitioner, not a commentator
- Identify any claims, framings, or language that would damage or dilute the brand
- Score brand alignment from 1–10

### Build Better Brand Attributes
Build Better is:
- **Practitioner-led.** Claims come from doing, not observing. Every piece of advice is one the creator has personally executed.
- **Anti-hype.** Build Better does not use inflated language, viral bait, or exaggerated promises. If a claim is made, it is backed by specific experience.
- **Useful over entertaining.** Content is designed to be actionable. Inspiration without application is not a Build Better output.
- **Direct.** No hedging. No "it depends." If there is a position, the script states it. If there genuinely is no single answer, the script explains why — it does not hide behind false balance.
- **Peer-to-peer.** The tone is of one founder talking to another — not a guru, not a coach, not a beginner. Equal to equal.

### Questions This Stage Asks
1. Does the script claim anything the creator has not personally done or experienced?
2. Does the script use any inflated, hype-driven language?
3. Is the tone that of a peer talking to a peer — or does it slip into "guru" mode?
4. Is every piece of advice actionable, not merely inspirational?
5. Are there any positions taken in the script that contradict Build Better's public stance on similar topics?

### Pass Criteria
- Score ≥ 7
- All claims are first-person and experience-backed
- No hype language or exaggerated outcomes
- Tone is peer-to-peer throughout
- At least one actionable element is present

### Fail Criteria
- Score ≤ 6
- Script makes claims that are not grounded in personal experience
- Script is purely inspirational with no actionable component
- Tone drifts into guru framing ("I'm going to teach you how to...")
- Script contains language inconsistent with Build Better's anti-hype positioning

### Optimization Checklist
- [ ] Replace any third-person or hypothetical claims with first-person, specific ones
- [ ] Remove or replace all inflated outcome language ("life-changing," "crush it," "10x")
- [ ] Reframe any "guru-to-student" tone as "founder-to-founder"
- [ ] Add at least one concrete, immediate action the viewer can take
- [ ] Check every "you should" against: does Build Better do this?

### Example Improvements

**Before:** "If you implement these strategies, you could potentially see massive growth in your account."

**After:** "I went from 200 to 8,000 followers in 90 days. Here's the exact system, in order."

**Why it works:** The revision is specific, personal, and makes a measurable claim. "Massive growth" is hype. "200 to 8,000 in 90 days" is a fact. The brand is built on facts.

## Stage 8 — Instagram Optimization Review

### Mission
Verify that the script meets Instagram's platform-specific requirements: runtime, format compliance, caption readiness, and hook placement for the platform's specific viewing behavior. This is the final technical gate before production.

### Responsibilities
- Calculate estimated runtime from word count
- Enforce the 45–60 second hard limit
- Verify all Instagram-native format requirements are met
- Confirm the caption is optimized for the platform
- Score Instagram optimization from 1–10

### Questions This Stage Asks
1. What is the estimated runtime at 130 wpm? Is it within 45–60 seconds?
2. Does the hook appear in the first 3 seconds of spoken content?
3. Is the CTA placed in the final 5 seconds?
4. Is the caption under 125 characters for the visible preview line?
5. Does the on-screen text reinforce the spoken content without duplicating it word-for-word?
6. Is there a clear entry point for someone watching without audio?

### Pass Criteria
- Score ≥ 7
- Estimated runtime: 45–60 seconds (hard limit 60 seconds)
- Hook in opening 3 seconds
- CTA in final 5 seconds
- Caption visible preview line ≤ 125 characters
- On-screen text adds value rather than repeating spoken lines verbatim

### Fail Criteria
- Score ≤ 6
- Script runtime exceeds 60 seconds (triggers Runtime Compression Protocol — see `OPTIMIZATION_PIPELINE.md`)
- Script runtime is under 40 seconds (triggers padding audit — content may have been over-compressed in earlier stages)
- Hook does not appear until after the 5-second mark
- No CTA present
- Caption is the script transcribed verbatim

### Optimization Checklist
- [ ] Count spoken words; calculate runtime at 130 wpm
- [ ] If runtime > 60 seconds: run Runtime Compression Protocol before proceeding
- [ ] If runtime < 40 seconds: review prior stage outputs for over-compression
- [ ] Confirm hook appears in opening sentence
- [ ] Confirm CTA is the final spoken element
- [ ] Write or revise caption so the first 125 characters function as a standalone hook
- [ ] Review on-screen text — remove any lines that exactly duplicate spoken content

### Example Improvements

**Runtime (overlong):** 170-word script at 130 wpm = ~78 seconds. Exceeds limit by 18 seconds. Run Runtime Compression Protocol. Target: remove 40–45 words while preserving all key points and emotional beats.

**Caption (wrong):** "In this video I break down the five things I learned about building an audience on social media and how you can apply them to your own content strategy."

**Caption (right):** "5 things I learned that most creators take 2 years to figure out. 👇"

**Why it works:** The revised caption creates a knowledge gap in 69 characters. It fits fully in the preview without truncation. It implies speed advantage (others take years), which is the share trigger from Stage 6.
